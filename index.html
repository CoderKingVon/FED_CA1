INCREASED TEMPERATURES

Background on Global Warming 🕐

In simple terms, global warming is the long-term warming of the planet’s overall temperature. This rate of warming has significantly increased over the years due to higher usage of fossil fuels. 🏭

Main Causes of Global Warming 🔥🌎

1. Generating power ⚡

Generating electricity and heat by burning fossil fuels causes a large chunk of global emissions. Most electricity is still generated by burning coal, oil, or gas, which produces carbon dioxide and nitrous oxide – powerful greenhouse gases that blanket the Earth and trap the sun’s heat.

2. Deforestation 🌲🪓

Cutting down forests to create farms or pastures, or for other reasons, causes emissions, since trees, when they are cut, release the carbon they have been storing. Each year approximately 12 million hectares of forest are destroyed.

Rising Sea Levels

Facts About Rising Sea Levels

What does rising sea level exactly mean?

Sea level rise is an increase in the level of the world’s oceans due to the effects of global warming. This means that the total volume of waters in the ocean has increased, and the average depth of the sea has increased.


What causes sea levels to rise? 📈

Sea level rise is caused primarily by two factors related to global warming:

(i) The added water from melting ice sheets and glaciers
(ii) The expansion of seawater as it warms.


Consequences ⚠

Sea level rise poses a serious threat to coastal life around the world. The intensity of floods, storm surges and damage to costal areas increases significantly.

Adverse Health Effects on Living Things ☠

1. Heat-related Mortality 🔥

Extreme heat is deadly. During a heatwave, the number of “heat-related deaths” – where exposure to heat either causes or significantly contributes to a death – tends to increase.

More than 61,000 people died as a result of searing heat across Europe in the summer of 2022. And a recent study found that more than one-third of all heat-related deaths recorded over 1991–2018 are linked to climate change.

2. Cold-related Mortality 🧊

Cold temperatures are a bigger problem than hot. But with climate change, hot temperatures will be more and more serious.

In Southeast Asia, approximately 190,000 annual deaths are caused by “non optimal temperatures”, with about 89 per cent of those being cold-related. But the trends are shifting in the region, with heat-related deaths on the rise.

3. Air Quality

Climate change can also affect indoor air quality. Increases in outside air pollutants, such as ozone and particulate matter, could lead to higher indoor exposures. These pollutants can enter a building in many ways, including through open doors and windows and ventilation systems.

Additionally, Indoor air pollutants have been linked to heart disease, respiratory diseases such as asthma, and cancer.

4. Ozone Depletion 🔵

The major ozone losses that have been observed in the lower stratosphere due to the human-produced chlorine- and bromine-containing gases have a cooling effect on the Earth's surface.

Gases, such as the chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are knowns to destroy the ozone layer.

Ozone depletion can cause increased amounts of UV radiation to reach the Earth which can lead to cases of skin cancer, cataracts, and impaired immune systems.

5. Dengue Fever 🦟

Warming temperatures and unprecedented flooding have also encouraged the spread of mosquitos well beyond their traditional breeding grounds, bringing deadly viruses.

If nothing is done, Zika Virus will threaten an additional 1.3 billion people by 2050, and dengue fever will impact 60 percent of the world’s population by 2080.

A Great Danger To Economy

1. Agriculture 🌱

Agriculture is most prone to climate change.

Increased heat and drought will likely reduce crop yields. According to a 2011 National Academy of Sciences report, for every degree Celsius the global thermostat rises, there will be a 5 to 15 percent decrease in overall crop production.

As a result, farmers struggle to stay afloat by finding ways to adapt to changing conditions, prices will likely increase and be passed along to consumers.

2. Infrastructure 🏠

Much of our society's critical infrastructure are at risk!

Extreme rainfall events have increased 37 percent in the Midwest since the 1950s, and this year, the region has experienced above normal amounts of rain and snowmelt that have caused historic flooding.

Did You Know?

Military bases are even vulnerable and communication systems are also at high risk.

3. Tourism ✈

Sea level rise could submerge small islands and coastal areas, while deforestation and its destructive impacts on biodiversity could make some tourist destinations less attractive.

Two billion dollars could be lost in winter recreation due to less snow and ice. For example, rapid warming in the Adirondack Mountains could decimate the winter activity sector, which makes up 30 percent of the local economy.

What Is Sustainable Development Goals?

Which Of the 17 SDGs Am I Focusing On?

Why Talk About SDG 13 and Not the Others?

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), were adopted by the United Nations in 2015 as a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure that by 2030 all people enjoy peace and prosperity.

The 17 SDGs are integrated — they recognize that action in one area will affect outcomes in others.

In the introduction page, you could see that I was talking about climate change and its consequnces. The particular SDG that specifically targets this problem is SDG 13.

SDG 13 is a climate action goal. Its aim is to get people around the globe to tackle climate change!

Climate action is of paramount importance in this day and age. This is because it affects on a global scale, where anyone in any corner can feel its impacts.

In the modern day, we can already feel the consequences of climate change already. We urgently need to take actions to combat this.

Let us have an overview of the government's plan for climate action in Singapore 🇸🇬 and in Denmark 🇩🇰

1. Singapore's Green Plan

The Singapore Green Plan 2030, or the Green Plan, is a whole-of-nation movement to advance Singapore’s national agenda on sustainable development.

The Green Plan charts ambitious and concrete targets over the next 10 years, strengthening Singapore’s commitments under the UN’s 2030 Sustainable Development Agenda and Paris Agreement, and positioning us to achieve our long-term net zero emissions aspiration by 2050.

2. Denmark: A Green and Sustainable World

1. Reduce global greenhouse gas emissions

2. Increase global climate ambition

3. Strengthen focus on adaptation and sustainable development
